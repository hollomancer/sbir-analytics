**Grain note (do not conflate):** the **28.1% / 71.9%** award-level split is a strict, financially grounded bar (coded Phase III, private raise, or recovered follow-on contract). Equating them would collapse indeterminacy to ~33% — a gap-sizing thought experiment, not the headline the brief uses.

## Background: Why This Is Hard to Measure

The SBIR program does not define a single commercialization metric. Phase III — in statute — encompasses both continued federal procurement and private-sector commercialization resulting from Phase II work. These two outcomes land in entirely different data systems and are nearly invisible to each other.

Federal procurement shows up (partially) in FPDS. Private commercialization shows up in SEC filings, venture databases, and acquisition records — none of which are systematically linked to SBIR award identifiers. This analysis bridges those systems using entity matching on firm names and UEIs, which is imperfect.

A second structural problem: there is no authoritative definition of a "nanotechnology" SBIR award. The NNI's FY2026 Budget Supplement reports approximately $230M in nanotech SBIR/STTR activity in FY2023, but the methodology behind that figure is not published. Text-based keyword matching over award titles and abstracts — the approach used here — captures a different, likely smaller, slice of the same population. The figures below should be read as lower bounds on the nanotech SBIR universe, not a complete census.

## Cohort Definition

Awards were identified through keyword and phrase matching over award title and abstract text, using 52 patterns anchored to specific nanotechnology terminology (nanoparticle, nanotube, graphene, quantum dot, atomic layer deposition, NEMS, and related terms). This produced **2,849 Phase II awards** from a base of 68,075 total Phase II records in SBIR.gov — approximately 4.2% of all Phase II activity.

Two alternative methods were also run:

- A proxy based on the repository's existing CET (Critical and Emerging Technology) keyword heuristic produced 650 awards. This is not a subset of the keyword cohort: the CET term list includes "carbon fiber," which the nanotech keyword list deliberately omits, so 217 of its 640 unique award IDs fall outside the keyword cohort. Patent validation retires this method for cohort duty — its carbon-fiber-only firms hold nanotech patents at half the cohort baseline (Finding 5).
- A USPTO patent-classification approach — CPC classes B82Y/B82B over PatentsView bulk data — identifies **481 Phase II firms** holding at least one nanotechnology-classified patent, covering **6,786 Phase II awards** at the firm level. Finding 5 shows this instrument functions half as a pre-award capability marker and half as a post-award outcome measure, so it serves as a validation and signal source rather than a competing cohort definition.

The spread between methods — 650, 2,849, or 6,786 awards depending on instrument and grain — illustrates how much cohort size depends on definitional choices. The NNI's implied universe ($230M ÷ typical Phase II size) sits within this span, suggesting NNI's agency-level identification captures solicitation-topic classifications that are invisible in abstract text.

## Finding 1: Two Distinct Commercialization Pathways

When transition signals are examined separately by agency, a clear pattern emerges:

| Agency | Nanotech Phase II awards | FPDS Phase III coded | Post-Phase II Form D | Either signal |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Department of Defense | 1,413 | 12.5% | 8.8% | 19.4% |
| Dept. of Energy | 364 | 11.3% | 6.3% | 17.3% |
| NASA | 258 | 8.1% | 7.8% | 14.7% |
| Dept. of Health & Human Services | 393 | 3.1% | 9.4% | 12.0% |
| National Science Foundation | 333 | 2.4% | **17.7%** | 19.2% |

DoD and DOE nanotech firms return to the government procurement system at roughly three to five times the rate of NSF and NIH firms. NSF nanotech firms, by contrast, raise private capital at seven times the rate they appear in FPDS Phase III records. The two signals are nearly independent across the full cohort: only 37 awards (1.3%) show both FPDS Phase III and temporally-filtered Form D activity.

A third signal — M&A activity detected through SEC EDGAR filings (`ma_definitive`, `ma_proxy`, `acquisition`, and related mention types) — is present for 434 awards (15.2%) at any confidence level, narrowing to 54 awards (1.9%) at high confidence. Unlike the FPDS and Form D signals, M&A evidence does not separate cleanly by agency; rates range from 9–17% across all major agencies, suggesting acquisition is not strongly pathway-dependent. High-confidence M&A signals should be treated as a floor: entity matching by firm name misses acquisitions that occurred under a renamed entity or below SEC reporting thresholds.

This divergence reflects real differences in market structure, not measurement error. DoD nanotech — hypersonic thermal materials, MEMS-based sensors, radar absorbers — typically has no commercial market outside defense procurement. NSF and NIH nanotech — drug delivery systems, photonic devices, biosensors — can attract venture capital and ultimately reach commercial or clinical markets. The SBIR program is succeeding differently in these two contexts; a single aggregate transition rate obscures both.

**What the numbers imply for private capital:** Among the 272 temporally-filtered Form D matches (131 unique firms), the per-firm median raise was approximately $9 million, with the 90th percentile approximately $90 million. The median lag from Phase II end to first offering was approximately two years — consistent with a product development gap between government-funded prototype and investor-ready asset. This lag has policy implications: evaluation windows shorter than three to four years will systematically undercount NSF and NIH commercialization activity.

A targeted search of SEC EDGAR filings for named large acquirers identifies **11 nanotech SBIR firms** whose acquisition by a defense or pharma/medtech prime is supported by EDGAR documentary evidence — representing approximately 0.8% of the 1,339 unique firms in the cohort, or 56 awards (2.0%). All confirmed matches cleared a temporal filter requiring that the EDGAR acquisition signal post-date the firm's Phase II end date. (The broader EDGAR detection sweep flagged 16 firms and 71 awards; the 5 additional firms did not clear the confidence threshold due to name ambiguity or insufficient corroborating evidence.)

Several of these are publicly confirmed transactions with SEC-documented acquisition prices: Mercury Systems acquired Physical Optics Corporation in December 2020 for approximately $310 million (all-cash, per 8-K press release); Bruker acquired Anasys Instruments in 2018 for approximately $32.3 million total consideration ($27M cash + $5.3M contingent, per Bruker FY2018 10-K notes); Luminex acquired EraGen Biosciences in June 2011 for $34 million cash (per Luminex 8-K press release); and PerkinElmer acquired Visen Medical in July 2010 for $23 million (per PerkinElmer FY2010 10-K). ICx Technologies acquired Nomadics in August 2005 (price not SEC-disclosed; FLIR subsequently acquired all of ICx in October 2010 for $228M); C.R. Bard acquired EKOS Corporation in 2010 (EKOS was private; acquisition price not in SEC filings); Cubic Corp acquired GATR Technologies in August 2020 (price not SEC-disclosed; Cubic went private in 2021).

**What makes this pathway distinctive.** Strategic acquisition is arguably the strongest commercialization outcome in the dataset — it represents the acquirer's judgment that the technology and team were worth purchasing outright. It is also the most invisible to standard metrics: once acquired, the firm's subsequent contracts and grants appear under the acquirer's name and UEI, not the original SBIR awardee. The acquired firms vanish from Phase III transition counts entirely. A nanotech firm acquired by Mercury Systems for $310 million looks identical, in FPDS, to a firm that dissolved after its Phase II ended.

**SBIR leverage: government investment and acquisition outcomes.** The table below places each acquisition in context of the firm's total federal SBIR investment (all phases, all years). The "leverage multiple" — acquisition value divided by cumulative SBIR funding — is confirmed from SEC filings for four firms; for the remainder, prices were either not disclosed, the acquired firm was private, or the acquirer subsequently went private before filing. The SBIR investment figure is total across all phases and years, not solely the Phase II awards that preceded the acquisition; for long-tenured firms like Physical Optics, the majority of government funding came during the firm's mature contracting years, not its early R&D phase.

| SBIR Firm | Total SBIR investment | Acquirer | Approx. acquisition year | Reported acquisition value | Leverage (value / SBIR) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Physical Optics Corporation | $564M | Mercury Systems | 2020 | ~$310M (8-K press release) | 0.55× |
| Intellisense Systems Inc | $164M | Mercury Systems | ~2024 | undisclosed | — |
| Nomadics, Inc. | $27M | ICx Technologies → FLIR | 2005 | not in SEC filings | — |
| SY Technology, Inc. | $9M | L3 Technologies | ~2005 | not in SEC filings | — |
| GATR Technologies | $10M | Cubic Corp | 2020 | not in SEC filings | — |
| KAI, LLC | $6M | L3Harris | unknown | undisclosed | — |
| Anasys Instruments Corp | $14M | Bruker | 2018 | ~$32.3M (FY2018 10-K notes) | 2.3× |
| EKOS Corporation | $3M | C.R. Bard | 2010 | not in SEC filings (private) | — |
| EraGen Biosciences | $5M | Luminex | 2011 | ~$34M (8-K press release) | 6.8× |
| Senior Scientific | $5M | Bruker | ~2014 | undisclosed | — |
| Visen Medical, Inc. | $4M | PerkinElmer | 2010 | ~$23M (FY2010 10-K notes) | 5.8× |

The four confirmed leverage multiples reveal two distinct regimes. Physical Optics — a large, mature, primarily defense-oriented SBIR firm — was acquired for 0.55× its cumulative SBIR investment, below one dollar returned for each dollar of federal R&D funding. The government's outlay was already recouped many times over in delivered contracts; the $310M acquisition price reflects residual capability and IP value, not an exit premium. The three pharma/medtech firms tell a different story: EraGen (6.8×), Visen (5.8×), and Anasys (2.3×) were each acquired at multiples well above their cumulative SBIR funding, reflecting acquirer willingness to pay a strategic premium for technology they could not replicate organically. EraGen's molecular diagnostics platform and Visen's in vivo imaging agents were genuinely novel assets; the $34M and $23M prices, respectively, against $5M and $4M in total SBIR investment, represent a 6–7× government leverage ratio.

The pattern is not universal — acquisition leverage depends on the firm type. Defense contractors whose primary product is continued deliverability to the federal customer are acquired close to or below their government-funded cost basis. Technology creators with cross-market intellectual property are acquired well above it. A policy framing that uses a single "SBIR leverage" figure will mix these two regimes and produce a number that is uninformative about either.

The caveat is significant: seven of the eleven firms have no SEC-documented price — either because the acquired firm was private, because the acquirer went private before disclosure, or because the transaction was structured as an asset purchase below reporting thresholds. The table understates acquisition value in aggregate; the true leverage ratio across all 11 firms is unknown but likely higher than the four confirmed cases suggest, given that confirmed cases skew toward larger disclosed transactions.

**Agency pattern.** The split is consistent with the broader findings. DoD-funded firms appear exclusively in the defense prime column; HHS-funded firms (NIH grants for biosensors, imaging agents, and diagnostic systems) flow to pharma and medtech primes. NSF nanotech firms that commercialize appear more likely to raise Form D capital than to be acquired by a prime, though sample sizes are too small for this to be a firm conclusion.

**Temporal lags.** The median time from earliest Phase II end date to confirmed acquisition across the 10 firms with known acquisition dates is approximately 7 years. The longest is EKOS Corporation, where a Phase II award in 1998 preceded C.R. Bard's acquisition of the firm in 2010 — a 12-year span. This reinforces the evaluation window finding from Finding 1: outcome windows of two or three years will miss the majority of acquisition-pathway commercialization events.

**Floor estimate caveat.** This analysis detects acquisitions that (1) required SEC filing because they involved a publicly traded acquirer, (2) were large enough to trigger disclosure, and (3) matched by the acquired firm's name at the time of the transaction. Acquisitions of renamed entities, asset purchases below disclosure thresholds, and acquisitions by private-equity-owned defense contractors that are not SEC filers are not captured. The 11 firms identified should be treated as a lower bound.

## Finding 3: The Disappearance Problem

Of the 2,373 nanotech Phase II awards that are old enough to have matured (award year ≤ 2022) and show no FPDS-coded Phase III activity, the following breakdown applies:

| Reason transition status is indeterminate | N awards | Share of indeterminate |
|---|---|---|
| Firm has no federal activity record post-award | 1,298 | 54.7% |
| Firm cannot be linked to federal systems (no UEI) | 539 | 22.7% |
| Firm in FPDS but no contract carries Phase III coding | 354 | 14.9% |
| Non-DoD agency where FPDS Phase III coding is sparse | 182 | 7.7% |

The dominant category — 54.7% of the indeterminate population — is firms that received a Phase II award and subsequently disappeared from federal procurement records entirely. This is not automatically a negative outcome. Some of these firms will be among those captured by the Form D signal; others will have been acquired (removing them from the SBIR awardee name match); others will have reached commercial markets without touching SEC reporting thresholds; some will have dissolved.

The current data infrastructure cannot distinguish between these outcomes. A firm that raised $200M in a private round and was acquired by a major corporation looks identical, in FPDS, to a firm that received a Phase II award and immediately went out of business. Both show up in the "firm activity absent" bucket.

Patent data now quantifies this bucket. Of the 651 firms behind these awards, 428 (66%) match a patent assignee of some class and 341 (52%) filed after their first award. Because exact name matching against the full assignee universe can collide on generic firm names, every match carries a confidence tier built from three corroboration signals (assignee-state agreement, inventor-vs-PI name overlap, name specificity). The result is decisive: **328 of 651 "disappeared" firms (50%) filed patent applications after their first Phase II award at high confidence** — 95% of high-confidence matches are corroborated by inventor↔PI name agreement (the same person who ran the firm's SBIR research appears as an inventor on the matched patents), the rest by state agreement on a distinctive name. Half of the population that federal spending data wrote off was demonstrably alive and developing IP, using nothing but free public data. The no-UEI bucket behaves the same way (131 of 368, 36%, at high confidence). Per-firm results with tiers: `data/nano_dark_firm_liveness.csv` (`scripts/data/nano_dark_firm_liveness.py`).

Trademarks push past liveness into direct commercialization evidence: a firm registering a product mark is going to market. Matching the same dark firms against USPTO trademark case files (2023 vintage), 51% own at least one mark and 43% hold a *registered* mark; **225 firms (35%) filed a trademark after their first Phase II award, 126 of them (19%) at high confidence** (state-corroborated, distinctive names). The marks themselves are product names — wound dressings, lidar systems, deposition tooling — from firms invisible to every federal award dataset (`scripts/data/nano_dark_firm_trademarks.py`, `data/nano_dark_firm_trademarks.csv`).

A final instrument closes a blind spot the others share: firms that renamed or were acquired defeat every *exact-name* match at once. Building a firm alias graph from USPTO-recorded name-change and merger conveyances (`sbir_etl/utils/firm_aliases.py`) and re-checking the dark firms under their successor names recovers **11 firms that were negative under their own name but demonstrably active under a new one** — including WaveBand Corporation (merged into the defense prime Sierra Nevada Corporation), Boulder Ionics (CoorsTek Fluorochemicals), and Microchip Biotechnologies (IntegenX), each carrying trademark, patent, and in three cases federal-contract evidence under the successor name. These are canonical transitions — in WaveBand's case, absorption into a prime — rendered invisible purely by a change of corporate identity (`scripts/data/nano_alias_expanded_evidence.py`).

The largest single contribution comes from a channel none of the prior instruments touched: subcontract (FSRS) data. Every prior check queried *prime* federal awards, but a canonical SBIR transition is delivering technology *through* a prime — becoming a subcontractor rather than a direct awardee — which is invisible in prime-award data and looks exactly like disappearance. Querying USAspending subawards by sub-awardee name for the 651 disappeared firms finds **117 (18%) with a post-award subaward, 96 at strong tier** (≥1 subaward to a scaled prime or ≥$100K total). Twenty-two of these are net-new — negative under every patent, trademark, and alias check — including firms subcontracting directly to Northrop Grumman, Lockheed Martin, and SRI International (`scripts/data/nano_ws5a_subawards.py`, `data/nano_ws5a_subawards.csv`). Coverage caveat: FSRS reporting has a documented ~$30K threshold and under-reporting, and coverage begins ~FY2011, so this is a floor.

The dollar volume behind that subaward evidence is worth sizing, with the same caution Finding 2 applied to acquisition prices. Across the 103 strong-tier firms (both dark buckets), post-award subaward volume totals $3.2B — but this is not evenly spread: two large, clearly-active engineering firms (Foster-Miller, $1.3B across 473 subawards; Technology Service Corp, $865M across 387) account for two-thirds of that sum, so the median firm ($1.4M) is the representative figure, not the total. Expressing this as leverage — subaward dollars against cumulative SBIR investment, the same ratio Finding 2 used for acquisition prices, though here it measures an ongoing supply relationship rather than a one-time exit — the typical firm is modest (median 0.18×), but **18% of firms (19 of 103) show subaward volume exceeding their entire cumulative SBIR investment**, and the 90th percentile reaches 3.4×. The largest multiples (Senspex 141×, Five Stones Research 63×) are driven by small SBIR-investment denominators (~$1.2M) rather than exceptional subaward scale, and should be read as amplified rather than representative. Four of the 103 firms — including Physical Optics — are already named in Finding 2's confirmed acquisitions; their subaward activity enriches that story rather than revising it (Physical Optics continued heavy Lockheed Martin, Raytheon, and Boeing subcontracting through 2021, consistent with integration into Mercury Systems' supply chain following its 2020 acquisition) (`scripts/data/nano_subaward_leverage.py`, `data/nano_subaward_leverage.csv`).

For the subset of disappeared firms funded by NIH/HHS — the pathway procurement data sees worst — two commercialization-specific federal registries add direct go-to-market evidence: of 206 HHS-funded dark firms, 24 are the lead sponsor of a registered clinical trial and 12 hold a cleared FDA 510(k) device (33 firms either way; 7 net-new beyond the patent and trademark checks), including Imbed Biosciences' Microlyte wound dressing and EraGen Biosciences' FLEXMAP 3D diagnostics platform (`scripts/data/nano_ws5c_sector_registries.py`, `data/nano_ws5c_sector_registries.csv`).

**Combined across every instrument** — patents, trademarks, aliases, subawards, and sector registries — **427 of the 651 disappeared firms (66%) now show some form of post-award activity evidence**, up from the 50% the patent check alone established. A fifth instrument, SAM.gov registration status, remains scoped but blocked: it needs a `SAM_GOV_API_KEY` this analysis does not hold (`scripts/data/nano_ws5b_sam_status.py` is ready to run once one is supplied).

A multi-list capture-recapture pass over this same evidence — run separately from the cohort-wide T10 analysis in Finding 5, and not comparable to it (different population: all 1,019 procurement-dark-or-no-UEI firms, not just the 651; different channels: patent, trademark, alias, subaward, with sector registries excluded because they were only searched for a 206-firm subset) — behaves in a genuinely different way. Where the cohort-wide pass self-rejected (the Chao bound exceeded the population size, meaning the channels are almost entirely disjoint), this one is well-behaved: 604 of 1,019 firms (59.3%) are caught by at least one channel, and the Chao lower bound is 824 (81%) — a real, if imprecise, estimate rather than a mathematical impossibility. The reason is co-occurrence: 38% of detected firms are caught by two or three channels rather than one (patent∩trademark alone overlaps on 232 firms), consistent with a genuine "still operationally active on multiple fronts" subpopulation within the dark majority. The alias channel is the exception, but by construction rather than discovery — it was only credited to firms already negative on patent and trademark, so its near-zero overlap with either is definitional, not evidence of true independence (`scripts/data/nano_capture_recapture.py`, `data/nano_capture_recapture_darkfirms.csv`).

The 22.7% entity resolution failure rate — awards in SBIR.gov that lack a UEI — reflects a data quality problem that predates the UEI transition and is particularly acute for pre-2012 awards. It is not permanently opaque: searching USAspending by firm *name* and requiring exact normalized-name equality resolves 157 of the 368 firms (43%) to candidate UEIs — 156 of them to a single unambiguous UEI — and evidence classification of their 247 awards finds 21 strong and 79 moderate (`scripts/data/nano_ws2_resolve_no_uei.py`, `data/nano_no_uei_resolution.csv`). The 57% that stay unresolved have no post-FY2008 federal footprint under any matching name, consistent with firms whose activity predates USAspending's visibility floor.

## Finding 4: The FPDS Coding Gap Is Structural, Not Incidental

For non-DoD agencies, the absence of FPDS Phase III coding is not evidence of failed transition — it is a known characteristic of how those agencies procure. GAO-24-106398 documents that FPDS Phase III flags are populated primarily by DoD contracting officers and are absent or inconsistent in NIH, NSF, and DOE procurement systems.

The practical effect: NIH and NSF nanotech firms that do receive subsequent federal awards — follow-on grants, cooperative agreements, BAA contracts — will generally not appear in Phase III contract counts. The 3.1% FPDS Phase III rate for NIH nanotech firms almost certainly understates the true federal re-engagement rate; it captures only the cases where a contracting officer specifically coded the Phase III flag, which is not standard NIH practice.

This matters for program evaluation. Using FPDS Phase III rates as a comparative metric across agencies systematically disadvantages programs — like NSF and NIH — whose procurement culture does not produce that coding. Any cross-agency comparison using this metric will misleadingly favor DoD.

**The gap is now quantified by contract-level recovery.** For the 536 cohort awards whose firms appear in federal data but carry no Phase III flag (the `NO_FPDS_CODING` and `DATA_GAP_FPDS_NONDOD` buckets), we pulled each firm's full post-award USAspending record and classified the evidence per award (`scripts/data/nano_ws1_contract_evidence.py`):

| Evidence tier | Definition | Awards |
|---|---|---|
| **Strong** | Post-award contract with explicit "Phase III" text (146) or a same-agency non-SBIR contract ≥ $25K (155) | **301 (56%)** |
| Moderate | Non-SBIR contract from a different agency, sub-$25K same-agency contract, or non-SBIR grant | 159 (30%) |
| Weak | Only later SBIR/STTR awards (continued participation, not transition) | 72 (13%) |
| None | No post-award federal actions | 4 (1%) — all Phase II end dates 2024–2026 |

The 146 explicit-marker awards are the starkest evidence: contracts whose *descriptions* literally say "SBIR PHASE III" — including a $10M Navy delivery order — but whose FPDS Phase III field is empty. Method notes: later SBIR awards are excluded by ID join against SBIR.gov (necessary because ~70% of DoD SBIR contract descriptions omit the token "SBIR"); Phase I/II language takes precedence over Phase III *mentions*; the evidence window starts FY2008 (USAspending's floor), so pre-2008 follow-ons are invisible and older awards are biased toward lower tiers.

**Effect on the headline numbers:** counting strong-tier recovery as observable commercialization evidence raises the combined observable signal from 17.4% to 27.5% of the cohort (28.1% once no-UEI identity resolution is added — Finding 3) and cuts the indeterminate share from 82.6% to **71.9%**. The flag-based figures remain the conservative floor; the recovered figures are the corrected view of the same population.

## Finding 5: The Patent Lens — Validation, Timing, and a Fifth Signal Channel

Executing the patent-classification method (USPTO CPC B82Y/B82B over PatentsView bulk data, with filing dates) identified 481 SBIR Phase II firms holding at least one nanotechnology-classified patent, covering 6,786 Phase II awards at the firm level. The patent lens did not end up serving as a third cohort definition. It did something more useful: it answered measurement questions the text-based methods could not answer about themselves, and it surfaced a new outcome signal.

**The keyword definition survives an adversarial test.** Sixty-one percent of patent-verified nanotech firms (293 of 481) never triggered the keyword net — which initially looks like a recall failure. Inspection of those firms' 1,416 award abstracts shows otherwise: only 10% contain even *near*-nano vocabulary (thin film, epitaxy, superlattice, plasmonics, quantum wells). The rest have no nano-adjacent language at all — these firms' SBIR awards are in other domains, and their nanotech IP comes from elsewhere in the business. The keyword cohort's misses are a construct difference (award content vs firm capability), not a vocabulary gap, and expanding the term list would sacrifice the precision that makes the cohort defensible.

**The CET category label fails its test.** The CET proxy's only unique contribution beyond the keyword method is its carbon-fiber-only awards — and those firms hold nanotechnology patents at 7%, *half* the keyword-cohort baseline of 14%. Carbon-fiber firms are less nano-patent-active than the population they were supposed to help identify. A category-label taxonomy that has not been validated against behavioral data can be empirically wrong in ways that silently distort counts.

**Patent timing splits capability from outcome.** On a filing-date basis, 50% of patent-holding firms filed their first nanotech application *after* their first Phase II award year, 42% before it, and 7% in the same year. (Grant dates — which lag filings by 2–4 years — would misleadingly suggest 74% post-award.) The two halves mean different things. Pre-award filers carry demonstrated nanotech IP capability into the program — a signal available at application time. Post-award first filings are commercialization-adjacent outcomes, positioning patent activity as a **fifth transition-signal channel** alongside FPDS coding, federal obligations, Form D, and M&A. It is also why the within-cohort association between patents and transition (patent-holding keyword firms: 6.4% firm-level FPDS Phase III vs 2.2% for non-holders) must be read as correlation among outcomes, not as a pre-award predictor — the association mixes both timing regimes.

**Patents are strongest where procurement data is weakest.** After de-graining to firms, patent holders show elevated private-market signals — Form D 23.5% vs 15.0%, medium+high M&A 16.6% vs 6.1% — while firm-level FPDS Phase III rates are indistinguishable (2.5% vs 2.8%; the award-level "4× gap" is a composition artifact of prolific multi-award firms and should not be cited). Patenting travels with the acquisition and private-capital pathways — the ones federal data systems see worst — consistent with Finding 2 (Physical Optics and Anasys, both acquired by primes, both hold B82 patents), and with Finding 3, where patent filings recover activity evidence for 328 "disappeared" firms.

**A high-confidence core exists.** The 742 unique awards that are both text-matched and from patent-verified firms show 23.7% FPDS-coded Phase III — 2.6 times the keyword-cohort baseline. Where a single headline number is unavoidable, it should come from this double-confirmed subset, with the definitional layering stated alongside it.

**Caveats.** Only 14% of keyword-cohort firms hold B82 patents — USPTO examiners under-assign the B82 classes and small firms frequently do not patent, so patents cannot serve as the sole definition either. Assignee-to-firm matching is exact on normalized names (high precision, unknown recall: renames, subsidiaries, and university assignees are missed). The "any federal obligation" channel under-measures the patent cohort because the underlying digest joins on UEI and patent-cohort firms skew older than UEI-era tracking. Same-year filings (7%) cannot be assigned to either timing regime at year granularity.

## What Is Not Visible

Several commercialization pathways are beyond the reach of this analysis:

**Direct commercial revenue.** A nanotech firm that licenses a patent, wins a commercial contract, or sells product to industrial buyers leaves no trace in federal data systems unless it files with the SEC. Most small firms do not.

**Acquisitions below reporting thresholds.** A $15M acquisition of a nanotech startup — common in the materials and biomedical device sectors — may not trigger SEC reporting and will not appear in the Form D data used here.

**Licensing and technology transfer.** University-adjacent SBIR recipients (particularly common in NSF and NIH nanotech) often commercialize through patent licensing agreements that generate no SBIR-traceable transaction. Post-award patent filings (Finding 5) partially illuminate this pathway — a filed patent is a precondition for licensing and is publicly observable even when the license itself is not.

**NNI-classified awards missed by keyword matching.** The gap between our 2,849-award keyword cohort and NNI's implied ~3,800–6,000 award universe (based on $230M at typical Phase II sizes) means a meaningful fraction of nanotech SBIR activity is not captured here. Awards where the nanotech scope is stated in a classified solicitation topic, or where the abstract uses domain-specific terminology without standard nano-vocabulary, will be missed. The patent method now confirms this empirically: 61% of patent-verified nanotech firms (293 of 481) are invisible to abstract-text matching (Finding 5).

## What This Says About Nanotechnology Commercialization

Read together, the findings support six bounded conclusions.

**1. Nanotechnology commercializes as an ingredient, not as a sector.** The pathway follows the application market rather than the technology: DoD- and DOE-funded nanotech returns to government procurement at three to five times the rate of NSF/NIH nanotech, NSF nanotech raises private capital at seven times the rate it appears in Phase III records, and the two populations barely overlap (1.3%; Finding 1). The firm data says the same thing: 293 firms hold nanotechnology patents while their SBIR awards are described in other domains entirely — nano capability diffuses through firm portfolios rather than defining them (Finding 5). Policy aimed at "the nanotech industry" targets something that does not exist as a unit; policy aimed at defense sensing, drug delivery, or semiconductor process technology targets real populations.

**2. "Disappearance" is mostly a counting artifact, not a fate.** Flag-based channels show 17.4% of awards with a hard commercialization signal — money changed hands, or a coded federal contract; recovering mislabeled and no-UEI awards lifts that to 28.1% (Findings 3–4). Below that strict floor, the remaining population is not one undifferentiated unknown but three tiers. Roughly two-thirds of the firms with no federal activity record show some form of continuing activity once patents, trademarks, subawards, renamed identities, and sector registries are stacked together; a formal estimator applied to that broader dark population (including no-UEI firms) suggests the true share detectable by these instruments may run closer to four in five (Finding 3). A further slice turns out to be firms already known to have exited successfully, mislabeled only because their identity changed. What is left after all of that — an estimated fifth or less of the specific population these instruments were run against — is where genuine attrition is most plausibly concentrated, and it is the only part of the picture that still requires the survey. Continuation-or-commercialization can now be demonstrated for most of the cohort; unrecoverable silence is real, but substantially smaller than the original 55%-disappeared headline implied.

**3. Value concentrates in IP-rich exits, and returns bifurcate by firm type — and the 11 confirmed acquisitions are demonstrably a floor, not a count.** The acquisition pathway is numerically small (11 confirmed prime acquisitions; 0.8% of cohort firms) but carries the strongest outcomes, in two regimes: defense service firms are absorbed near or below their cumulative SBIR funding (Physical Optics: ~$310M against $564M, 0.55×), while technology creators exit at premiums (EraGen 6.8×, Visen 5.8×, Anasys 2.3×; Finding 2). The patent data corroborates this independently: patent-holding firms show two to three times the M&A and Form D rates of non-holders but identical FPDS Phase III rates (Finding 5). Markets and acquirers reward nanotech IP; procurement rewards deliverability. The rename graph confirms the undercount is not hypothetical: WaveBand Corporation's absorption into Sierra Nevada Corporation is invisible to every SEC/EDGAR-based instrument this analysis used — it appears in the raw EDGAR scan with zero M&A-type signal, is absent from the enriched M&A pipeline built on that scan, and Sierra Nevada was never in the curated list of primes searched, because a privately-held acquirer generates none of the SEC disclosure the whole detection method depends on (Finding 3). Any acquisition by a private or private-equity-owned prime is invisible to this methodology categorically, not occasionally.

**4. Nanotech commercialization is slower than any standard evaluation window.** Median two years from Phase II end to first private raise (Finding 1), roughly seven years to acquisition with a twelve-year tail (Finding 2), and half of patent-holding firms filed their first nanotech application only after their award era began (Finding 5). Nanotech sits at the deep-tech end of SBIR — physical-science product cycles, regulatory gates on the biomedical side, qualification cycles on the defense side. Annual or biennial assessments will structurally undercount the field; four to seven years is the realistic horizon.

**5. The program appears to build nanotech capability, not just rent it.** Half of the patent-holding firms first filed nanotech IP after entering the program — consistent with SBIR generating new capability rather than only subsidizing firms that already had it, though this is correlation, and the pre-award and same-year filers show that existing capability also flows in (Finding 5). Together with the leverage bifurcation, the fair characterization is that SBIR nanotech runs two different successful programs under one name: a defense-industrial capability pipeline whose commercialization is sustained federal absorption, and a venture-adjacent IP-seeding program whose commercialization is private capital and strategic exit. A single average misrepresents both.

**6. A third track exists alongside procurement and private capital: absorption into a prime's supply chain, invisible to every metric this program currently uses.** 18% of procurement-dark firms carry a post-award federal subaward, several delivering technology directly to Northrop Grumman, Lockheed Martin, and SRI International — not a fringe result, but 103 firms with $3.2B in aggregate post-award subcontract volume (concentrated in a few large, clearly-active engineering firms; the median firm's figure is $1.4M). For roughly one in six of these firms, cumulative subaward revenue already exceeds the government's entire cumulative SBIR investment in them (Finding 3). This is categorically different from the acquisition pathway in Finding 2 — a recurring supply relationship, not a one-time exit — and it is invisible by construction, not by data-access gap: a subcontractor never appears as the recipient of record on the prime's award, so no improvement in FPDS Phase III coding or prime-award search could ever surface it. A small technology firm quietly becoming a permanent supplier to a major defense prime is arguably as strong an SBIR outcome as winning a Phase III contract outright; the program has no mechanism to see it.

The measurement lesson is itself a finding about commercialization: the phenomenon is only as visible as the instruments observing it. Procurement data sees the defense half, SEC data sees the venture half, patent data sees the IP half — and each half looks like failure through the other's lens. Capture-recapture analysis makes this quantitative: 88% of firms detected by any channel are detected by exactly one, so the channels are complements, not replicates — each new instrument mostly finds new firms rather than confirming known ones, and no number of positive channels can establish how much commercialization remains unseen. Restricting the same test to the population that is dark on every one of those primary channels flips the result: there, the secondary "is it alive" instruments (patent, trademark, subaward) co-occur substantially rather than staying disjoint, and the estimator is well-behaved instead of self-rejecting (Finding 3). Read together, this suggests the dark majority is not one undifferentiated unknown — part of it is a coherent, still-active subpopulation that enough instruments would eventually illuminate, and part of it is a residual that none of the instruments deployed here can reach at all. Only the second part needs the survey.

## Policy Implications

**For leaders:** use [`nanotech_sbir_policy_brief.md`](nanotech_sbir_policy_brief.md) § What policy leaders should take away and § Language to avoid. The numbered items below are the technical cross-reference that the brief compresses.

**1. Aggregate transition rates are uninformative for nanotech.** The split between government-procurement and private-capital pathways is large enough that a single program-wide number will be misleading regardless of how it is computed. Reporting should distinguish DoD from non-DoD, and procurement-pathway from commercial-pathway, as a minimum.

**2. Evaluation windows need to match commercialization timelines — per pathway, not overall.** The channels run at different speeds: dateable procurement follow-ons arrive fast (71% of first signals within two years of Phase II end — the federal customer already exists), private raises lag a median of two years, and prime acquisitions run a median of seven. A time-to-first-signal survival analysis (`data/analysis/nano_time_to_signal_km.png`) shows roughly 30% of eventually-dateable signals arrive after year two. Annual or biennial assessments therefore capture most procurement transitions while systematically undercounting NIH/NSF private-pathway and acquisition outcomes; pathway-specific windows (two years for procurement, four to five for private capital, seven-plus for exits) fit the data better than any single window.

**3. The entity resolution gap is a data infrastructure problem — and a fixable one.** Nearly one in four nanotech Phase II awards lacking Phase III evidence could not be assessed because the SBIR.gov record carries no UEI. Name-based resolution against USAspending recipients recovered unambiguous candidate UEIs for 43% of these firms with no new data infrastructure at all (Finding 3) — a floor on what systematic retroactive UEI backfill by SBA could achieve, since SBA holds registration details (addresses, EINs) unavailable to outside matching. A related but distinct failure mode is identity *persistence*, not identity absence: a firm that renamed or was absorbed via merger keeps a valid UEI throughout, just under a name nobody thought to search. The rename graph built for this analysis from public USPTO name-change and merger records recovers firms of exactly this kind (Finding 3); SBA could maintain an equivalent successor-name registry directly from its own award and entity data, without needing any external source.

**4. FPDS Phase III coding is not a cross-agency metric.** Using FPDS-coded Phase III rates to compare NSF or NIH programs against DoD programs will produce misleading results by design. A more appropriate cross-agency metric would combine FPDS activity (contracts and grants, not just Phase III-flagged contracts) with Form D, capital events, and post-award patent filings, reported separately by pathway.

**5. Prime-contractor subcontracting is a legitimate SBIR outcome that current federal tracking cannot see by design.** 18% of the 651 procurement-dark firms (117) show a post-award federal subaward, several delivering technology directly to Northrop Grumman, Lockheed Martin, and SRI International. This is not a data-access gap that a better search would fix: FPDS Phase III flags and prime-award search structurally cannot see a subcontractor, because the subcontractor never appears as the recipient of record on the prime's award. A firm absorbed into a defense prime's supply chain — arguably one of the strongest outcomes a small technology firm can have — is indistinguishable, in every metric this program currently uses, from a firm that dissolved. Agencies evaluating SBIR outcomes should incorporate FSRS subaward data as a first-class, routinely-pulled signal alongside prime-award activity, not a research afterthought.

**6. The "disappeared" population should be worked down a triage ladder, not surveyed blind.** The 1,298 awards (651 firms) with no post-award federal activity record represent the largest single uncertainty in this analysis — and the dark population as a whole decomposes into four distinct problems with four distinct treatments (see `specs/dark-majority-resolution/`): awards mislabeled by missing Phase III flags are recoverable now from contract-level federal data; no-UEI awards need retroactive entity resolution; too-recent awards need survival-analysis framing, not failure counts. For the genuinely procurement-dark remainder, escalate through cheap public instruments before fielding anything expensive: patent liveness, trademark filings, subcontract data, and an identity-alias graph (all done — combined, 66% of firms show post-award activity evidence at zero marginal cost); sector registries for the biomed slice (done — clinical trials, FDA clearances); SAM.gov registration status (scoped, blocked on a credential this analysis does not hold); state corporate registry status and web/domain liveness (deferred — the former is the only negative-evidence source short of fielding the survey); and only then a 50–100 firm survey on the residual, stratified by everything the instruments found. Two capture-recapture passes clarify where that residual actually is. Across the full cohort's five primary channels, the estimator self-rejects (287 of 325 detected firms appear in exactly one channel; the Chao bound exceeds the cohort size) — the channels are almost entirely disjoint. Restricted to just the dark population using the four secondary instruments applied there uniformly, it does not self-reject: the Chao lower bound is 81% against 59% directly observed, because these secondary signals substantially co-occur (38% of detected firms hit by two or three channels). That contrast is the targeting logic for the survey: it belongs on the roughly one-fifth to two-fifths of dark firms these instruments never touch, not on firms already partially illuminated by them.

**7. Post-award patenting belongs in the standard transition-evidence toolkit.** Patent filings are public, machine-readable, attributable to firms, and impose zero reporting burden on small businesses — unlike commercialization surveys. In this cohort they recovered activity evidence for firms invisible to procurement data (Finding 3), travel with the acquisition and private-capital pathways that federal systems see worst (Finding 5), and partially illuminate the licensing pathway that no federal dataset observes. The method generalizes: every critical-and-emerging-technology area has CPC classes, so agencies evaluating SBIR outcomes in quantum, biotech, or advanced materials can add technology-relevant patent filings as a routine signal channel with one bulk-data pipeline — and trademark filings, which mark actual go-to-market events, come from the same portal for the same near-zero cost. A rename graph built from patent name-change and merger records (also public) closes the identity blind spot every exact-name match shares — it recovered firms here that had transitioned into acquirers, including a defense prime, under new names. The main caveats — examiner under-assignment of niche classes and name-matching through renames and acquisitions — parallel those already accepted for SEC-based signals.

**8. Technology-area counts are instrument-dependent; publish the instrument.** "Nanotech SBIR" is 650, 2,849, or 6,786 awards within this single analysis, depending on whether the instrument is a category label, award text, or firm patenting — and the NNI's own count implies a fourth, unpublished method. None of these is wrong; they answer different questions (program-category reporting, budget attribution, capability mapping). Cross-agency or year-over-year comparisons are meaningful only when the instrument and the grain (award-level vs firm-level) are stated with the number. Where a category taxonomy is used, validate its terms against behavioral data first: one term in the CET nanotech set ("carbon fiber") fails exactly that validation here, and unvalidated terms silently distort whatever is counted downstream.

## Methodological Notes

All figures in this report are provisional and subject to the following limitations:

- **Cohort definition:** Keyword matching over text fields will miss nanotech awards where the abstract does not use standard terminology. The NNI universe may be substantially larger.
- **FPDS Phase III undercounting:** GAO-24-106398 documents this as a structural problem, concentrated outside DoD. FPDS-coded rates should be treated as floors.
- **Form D matching:** Entity matching is by firm name and is imperfect. False positives (two different firms with similar names) and false negatives (name changes after acquisition) both occur. The high-confidence tier used here applies a multi-signal scoring algorithm (name, person, state, temporal), but exact attribution to specific Phase II awards is not possible.
- **NNI reference figures:** The agency-by-year NNI Table 5 figures used in the underlying reconciliation analysis are approximate public summary values. The exact NNI methodology for identifying nanotech SBIR awards is not published.
- **CPC/B82 patent method:** Built from the USPTO PatentsView PVGPATDIS release (extract built 2026-07-11): 63,287 B82Y/B82B patents, 7,510 unique assignee organizations, with application filing dates for 63,286. Assignee organizations are matched to SBIR firm names by exact match on normalized names — high precision, unknown recall (renames, subsidiaries, and university assignees are missed; USPTO examiners under-assign B82 classes). The patent cohort is firm-grained; its award-level rates are not comparable to text-cohort rates without de-graining. Timing claims use filing dates, not grant dates, at year granularity (methodology doc §5C–§5E).
- **WS1 contract-level recovery:** Evidence tiers come from USAspending prime-award records per recipient UEI (cached responses in `data/api_cache/usaspending_ws1/`), temporally filtered to actions starting after each award's Phase II end date. Later SBIR/STTR awards are excluded by normalized-ID join against SBIR.gov; Phase I/II description language takes precedence over Phase III mentions; same-agency contracts under $25K do not count as strong. The evidence window begins FY2008 (USAspending search floor) — follow-on activity before 2008 is invisible, biasing pre-2006 awards toward lower tiers. Strong-tier evidence means a continued federal customer relationship, not a verified Phase III designation.
- **WS2 identity resolution:** No-UEI firms are searched in USAspending by name; candidates require exact normalized-name equality with the SBIR firm, and confidence reflects UEI uniqueness (high = one UEI). Evidence tiers for resolved firms reuse the WS1 classifier. Name churn (renamed or acquired firms) produces false negatives; the FY2008 visibility floor leaves genuinely pre-2008 activity unresolvable by this route.
- **Dark-firm patent liveness:** Any-class assignee matches carry confidence tiers from three corroboration signals — assignee-state vs award-state agreement, inventor last names vs PI last names, and name specificity against a generic-token stoplist. In the current run 95% of high-confidence matches are inventor↔PI corroborated and the rest state-corroborated with distinctive names (SBIR.gov full state names are normalized to USPS codes before comparison). "Filed post-award" compares latest matched filing year to the firm's first Phase II award year — activity evidence, not survival to present.
- **Dark-firm trademarks:** Matched against USPTO Trademark Case Files (TRCFECO2, 2023 research vintage — filings after 2023 are invisible) by exact normalized owner-name equality; registration status treats `registration_no` of all zeros as never-registered; confidence uses state agreement + name specificity (trademark owner records carry no person names, so no PI signal exists). Filing a mark evidences going to market; it does not by itself prove revenue.
- **Firm alias graph:** Built from USPTO patent-assignment conveyances (ECORSEXC 2023) filtered to `namechg`/`merger` types, which are documentary (USPTO-recorded) and trusted directly; shared-UEI aliases from the WS1/WS2 USAspending caches are weak and require independent evidence under the alias name before counting. Chains of renames resolve via connected components. Coverage is limited to firms whose rename/merger involved a USPTO patent-portfolio transfer — firms that renamed without any IP transfer are not covered.
- **Subaward (FSRS) evidence:** USAspending subaward search by exact normalized sub-awardee name, temporally filtered to actions after the firm's earliest Phase II end date; tier logic mirrors WS1 (§ Finding 4). FSRS carries a documented ~$30K reporting threshold and known under-reporting, and subaward coverage begins ~FY2011 — both understate the true subcontracting rate, especially for pre-2011 awards.
- **Sector registries (biomed slice):** ClinicalTrials.gov (v2 API) lead-sponsor matches and openFDA 510(k) applicant matches, both exact-normalized-name (alias-expanded), scoped to HHS-funded dark firms only (206 of 651). A trial or clearance under a *different* sponsoring entity (e.g., a licensee) would not match; this is a floor on go-to-market activity, not a ceiling.
- **SAM.gov status (WS5b):** Scoped and implemented (`scripts/data/nano_ws5b_sam_status.py`) but not run — requires a `SAM_GOV_API_KEY`, which this analysis does not hold and which cannot be self-issued (SAM.gov keys are tied to a login). The script exits cleanly with instructions rather than producing partial output.
- **Subaward leverage:** Cumulative SBIR investment sums `Award Amount` from `award_data.csv` across ALL of a firm's SBIR.gov records (all phases, all years — not limited to the nanotech cohort), matching Finding 2's acquisition-leverage methodology exactly. "Leverage" here measures an accumulating supply relationship, not a one-time exit price, and should not be read as directly comparable to Finding 2's acquisition multiples despite the shared framing. Firms already named in Finding 2's confirmed acquisitions are flagged and excluded from "newly characterized" framing (`scripts/data/nano_subaward_leverage.py`, `data/nano_subaward_leverage.csv`).
- **Dark-population capture-recapture:** A second pass of `scripts/data/nano_capture_recapture.py`, run over the 1,019-firm dark population (not the full 2,849-award cohort) using only the four secondary instruments applied uniformly there (patent, trademark, alias, subaward). Not comparable to the cohort-wide five-channel pass in Finding 5 — different population, different channels, sector registries excluded (206-firm subset only). The alias×patent and alias×trademark near-zero overlaps are a construction artifact (alias recovery required prior patent/trademark negativity), not evidence of channel independence.
- **Survival analysis:** The time-to-first-signal curve (`data/analysis/nano_time_to_signal_km.png`) is a Kaplan–Meier estimate over dateable channels only (post-Phase-II Form D, strong contract-level evidence, post-award first B82 filing); FPDS-coded Phase III and M&A signals carry no reliable per-award dates and are excluded, so the curve understates total observable signal and should be read for its time profile, not its plateau. Awards without signals are right-censored at 2026-07; awards from the last three years are censored observations throughout the report (threshold advances automatically on re-run).
- **Capture-recapture bound:** Multi-list analysis at firm grain (`scripts/data/nano_capture_recapture.py`) over five channels (FPDS-coded, post-P2 Form D, strong contract evidence, high-confidence M&A, post-award B82 filing). The capture-count distribution is singleton-dominated (287/31/6/1 firms detected by 1/2/3/4 channels), the WS1 channel's overlap with FPDS coding is zero by construction, and the Chao heterogeneity-robust bound exceeds the cohort size — the multi-list model is inconsistent with channels sampling one common population, so no capture-recapture upper bound is reportable. Defensible statements: observed floor 24.3% of firms (28.1% of awards); ceiling unbounded short of negative evidence.
- **SEC EDGAR M&A scan:** The full scan of 34,460 SBIR firms in `sec_edgar_scan.jsonl` is complete and covers 99.9% of the nanotech cohort. A separate recent scan run wrote a summary showing zero detections due to repeated HTTP 500 errors — that summary reflects a failed process, not the underlying data. M&A mention signals in this report draw on the complete scan, filtered to acquisition-specific mention types (`ma_definitive`, `ma_proxy`, `acquisition`, `subsidiary`, `ownership_active`). Confidence tiers reflect multi-signal scoring from the enrichment pipeline: high-confidence M&A signals are present for 54 awards (1.9%); medium for 166 (5.8%); low for 214 (7.5%). Only high-confidence signals should be cited in formal contexts.

Full methodology, term lists, confidence tags, and reproducible code are in `docs/nano_phase3_methodology.md` and `scripts/data/build_nano_cohort.py`.

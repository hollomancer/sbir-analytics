# Nanotechnology SBIR/STTR Phase II Outcomes: What Policy Leaders Can Safely Conclude

**Prepared for:** S&T policy leaders and NSET Subcommittee reviewers  
**Status:** Provisional; figures are bounded estimates, not final program rates  
**Data through:** FY2025 SBIR.gov awards, FY2024 USAspending records, SEC EDGAR scans, and the March 2026 PatentsView release  
**Technical appendix:** [`nanotech_sbir_transition_findings.md`](nanotech_sbir_transition_findings.md), [`nano_phase3_methodology.md`](nano_phase3_methodology.md)

## Bottom line

This review reframes the PR #428 nanotechnology report around the decisions policy leaders need to make. The evidence supports a clear conclusion: nanotechnology SBIR commercialization is real, but it is split across several channels that no single federal metric can see.

For the 2,849 Phase II awards identified by nanotechnology terms in award titles and abstracts:

| Measure | Result | How to use it |
|---|---:|---|
| FPDS-coded Phase III contracts | 9.2% of awards | Conservative government-procurement floor; strongest for DoD |
| Post-Phase-II Form D private raises | 9.5% of awards | Private-capital signal; strongest for NSF-funded firms |
| Awards with both FPDS and Form D evidence | 1.3% of awards | Shows the two pathways mostly identify different firms |
| Any flag-based FPDS/Form D signal | 17.4% of awards | Observable floor, not a transition rate |
| Observable after contract-level recovery and no-UEI identity resolution | 28.1% of awards | Better estimate of documented post-award activity |
| Still indeterminate after recovery | 71.9% of awards | Measurement gap; should not be read as failure |

The main policy implication is that an aggregate “Phase III transition rate” is not rigorous for this technology area. Procurement, private investment, acquisition, patents, and subcontracting each illuminate different outcomes. Agencies should report them separately and clearly state the evidence channel behind each number.

## What changed in this clarity and rigor pass

The original draft contained strong evidence but asked non-technical readers to hold too many methodological distinctions at once. This revision makes four changes:

1. **Separates facts, interpretations, and policy implications.** Percentages are presented as evidence-channel observations before any judgment is made about program performance.
2. **Uses “observable evidence” instead of “commercialization rate” unless the measure can support a rate claim.** This avoids overstating what linked administrative data can prove.
3. **Makes uncertainty operational.** The indeterminate group is decomposed into data-quality problems, timing/censoring, identity-resolution limits, and genuinely unresolved cases.
4. **Defines the decision standard for leaders.** The report now says what can be used for cross-agency comparison, what cannot, and what data infrastructure would improve measurement.

## Finding 1: Nanotechnology has two visible commercialization pathways, not one average outcome

DoD and DOE nanotechnology awards are more likely to reappear in federal procurement records. NSF-funded nanotechnology firms are more likely to raise private capital. These are different commercialization pathways, not competing versions of the same outcome.

| Agency | Nanotech Phase II awards | FPDS-coded Phase III | Post-Phase-II Form D | Either signal |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Department of Defense | 1,413 | 12.5% | 8.8% | 19.4% |
| Department of Energy | 364 | 11.3% | 6.3% | 17.3% |
| NASA | 258 | 8.1% | 7.8% | 14.7% |
| Health and Human Services | 393 | 3.1% | 9.4% | 12.0% |
| National Science Foundation | 333 | 2.4% | 17.7% | 19.2% |

Only 37 awards, or 1.3% of the cohort, show both FPDS-coded Phase III evidence and a post-Phase-II Form D filing. That small overlap is a substantive result: firms commercializing through government procurement are usually not the same firms commercializing through venture-backed private markets.

**Policy interpretation.** A single nanotechnology transition rate would mix defense procurement, biomedical commercialization, energy applications, and platform technologies into one misleading average. Reporting should distinguish at least two pathways: federal-customer continuation and private-market capitalization.

## Finding 2: Acquisition evidence is high value but structurally undercounted

The PR identifies 11 nanotechnology SBIR firms with documentary evidence of acquisition by defense, pharma, or medtech primes. This is a small share of the cohort, but it is a strong form of commercialization evidence because an acquirer is paying for technology, talent, intellectual property, or delivery capacity.

| SBIR firm | Acquirer | Domain | SEC-disclosed value |
|---|---|---|---:|
| Physical Optics Corporation | Mercury Systems | Defense electronics and photonics | ~$310M |
| Anasys Instruments Corp | Bruker | Nano-IR and AFM-IR instrumentation | ~$32.3M |
| EraGen Biosciences | Luminex | Molecular diagnostics | ~$34M |
| Visen Medical, Inc. | PerkinElmer | In vivo fluorescence imaging | ~$23M |

The leverage multiple differs by firm type. Mature defense contractors can be acquired below cumulative SBIR funding because much of the government value was already delivered through contracts. IP-rich biomedical and instrumentation firms can be acquired at several times cumulative SBIR funding because the acquirer is purchasing a scalable asset.

**Rigor caveat.** The 11 acquisitions are a floor, not a full count. Acquisitions by private firms, private-equity-owned primes, renamed entities, or transactions below SEC reporting thresholds will often be invisible to EDGAR-based methods.

## Finding 3: “No signal” is not the same as “no commercialization”

The most important measurement problem is the large indeterminate population. Among mature awards without FPDS-coded Phase III activity, the report identifies several reasons a transition might not be visible:

| Indeterminate reason | Awards | Share of indeterminate group |
|---|---:|---:|
| No federal activity record post-award | 1,298 | 54.7% |
| No UEI link to federal systems | 539 | 22.7% |
| Firm appears in FPDS, but no contract is Phase III coded | 354 | 14.9% |
| Non-DoD agency where FPDS coding is sparse | 182 | 7.7% |

This taxonomy is more rigorous than treating all missing evidence as failure. Some firms have public traces outside prime-award systems: patents, trademarks, subawards, clinical activity, name changes, or acquisition records. Others remain unresolved and require targeted outreach.

**Policy interpretation.** The right next step is not a broad survey of all firms. It is a triage ladder: first fix entity resolution, then recover unflagged contract evidence, then add public-record signals, and only then survey the residual group that remains dark.

## Finding 4: FPDS Phase III coding cannot support cross-agency rankings

FPDS Phase III coding is most useful for DoD-heavy procurement pathways. It is not a neutral cross-agency outcome metric. NIH, NSF, DOE, and other agencies may support commercialization through grants, cooperative agreements, follow-on R&D, private capital, licenses, or acquisitions that are not consistently captured by the FPDS Phase III field.

The PR’s contract-level recovery is therefore important. For 536 awards whose firms appeared in federal records but lacked Phase III flags, post-award USAspending review found:

| Evidence tier | Meaning | Awards |
|---|---|---:|
| Strong | Explicit Phase III text or same-agency non-SBIR contract of at least $25K | 301 |
| Moderate | Other non-SBIR federal activity | 159 |
| Weak | Later SBIR/STTR only | 72 |
| None | No post-award federal actions | 4 |

This substantially changes the interpretation. The absence of the Phase III flag is often a coding gap, not an absence of follow-on activity.

**Policy interpretation.** FPDS-coded Phase III should be reported as a conservative floor. Cross-agency dashboards should include separate columns for coded Phase III, unflagged federal follow-on obligations, private capital, patents, acquisitions, and subawards.

## Finding 5: Patents help validate the cohort and reveal outcomes that procurement data misses

The patent analysis uses CPC B82Y/B82B classes from PatentsView. It identifies 481 SBIR Phase II firms with at least one nanotechnology-classified patent. This should not replace the award-text cohort because it is firm-grained: a firm may hold nanotechnology patents while a particular SBIR award was in another domain.

The patent lens is useful in three ways:

- **Validation.** It tests whether the keyword cohort is meaningfully nanotechnology-related.
- **Timing.** About half of patent-holding firms filed their first nanotechnology application after the first Phase II award year, suggesting post-award capability development; pre-award filers instead indicate existing capability.
- **Signal discovery.** Patent-holding firms show stronger private-capital and M&A signals than non-patent holders, while FPDS Phase III rates are similar at the firm level.

**Rigor caveat.** Patents are an imperfect signal. Small firms may not patent, examiners may under-assign B82 classes, and exact-name assignee matching misses subsidiaries, renames, and university-linked commercialization paths.

## What policy leaders should take away

1. **Do not ask for one nanotechnology SBIR transition rate.** Ask for channel-specific outcomes with stated coverage limits.
2. **Do not compare NSF or NIH to DoD using FPDS-coded Phase III alone.** That comparison rewards procurement-system visibility rather than commercialization performance.
3. **Use longer evaluation windows.** Private raises and acquisitions often arrive several years after Phase II; short annual or biennial windows undercount deep-tech outcomes.
4. **Treat identity resolution as infrastructure.** Retroactive UEI backfill and successor-name tracking would recover evidence without imposing new reporting burdens on firms.
5. **Make subawards and patents standard evidence channels.** These signals are public, machine-readable, and especially useful where prime-contract records are weak.
6. **Reserve surveys for the unresolved residual.** Administrative and public-record checks should narrow the population before agencies impose survey burden.

## Language to avoid in final publication

For clarity and rigor, the final report should avoid these phrases unless narrowed:

- Avoid: “the nanotechnology transition rate is 17.4%.”  
  Use: “17.4% of awards show one of the two strict flag-based signals measured here.”
- Avoid: “71.9% did not commercialize.”  
  Use: “71.9% remain indeterminate after the recovery checks applied here.”
- Avoid: “patents prove commercialization.”  
  Use: “post-award patent filings are commercialization-adjacent evidence and a useful signal for technology development.”
- Avoid: “FPDS shows agency performance.”  
  Use: “FPDS-coded Phase III is a procurement-system signal whose coverage differs sharply by agency.”

## Recommended publication structure

A policy-leader version should be organized as follows:

1. One-page executive summary with the headline table.
2. Evidence-channel findings: procurement, private capital, acquisition, patents, subawards.
3. Measurement limits and why they matter for policy.
4. Recommended agency reporting standard.
5. Technical appendix with cohort definitions, matching rules, confidence tiers, and reproducibility notes.

This structure preserves the rigor of the PR #428 analysis while making the central message legible to senior S&T decision-makers: nanotechnology SBIR outcomes are distributed across markets and data systems, so policy should measure a portfolio of pathways rather than force all commercialization into a single Phase III count.

# Nanotechnology SBIR/STTR Phase II Outcomes: What Policy Leaders Can Safely Conclude

**Prepared for:** S&T policy leaders
**Status:** Provisional; figures are bounded estimates, not final program rates
**Data through:** FY2025 SBIR.gov awards, FY2024 USAspending records, SEC EDGAR scans, and the March 2026 PatentsView release
**Technical appendix:** [`nanotech_sbir_transition_findings.md`](nanotech_sbir_transition_findings.md), [`nano_phase3_methodology.md`](nano_phase3_methodology.md)

## Bottom line

Nanotechnology SBIR commercialization is real, but it is split across several channels that no single federal metric can see. For the 2,849 Phase II awards identified by nanotechnology terms in award titles and abstracts:

| Measure | Result | How to use it |
|---|---:|---|
| FPDS-coded Phase III contracts | 9.2% of awards | Conservative government-procurement floor; strongest for DoD |
| Post-Phase-II Form D private raises | 9.5% of awards | Private-capital signal; strongest for NSF-funded firms |
| Awards with both FPDS and Form D evidence | 1.3% of awards | Shows the two pathways mostly identify different firms |
| Any flag-based FPDS/Form D signal | 17.4% of awards | Observable floor, not a transition rate |
| Observable after contract-level recovery and no-UEI identity resolution | 28.1% of awards | Better estimate of documented post-award activity |
| Still indeterminate after recovery | 71.9% of awards | Measurement gap; should not be read as failure |

The main policy implication: an aggregate "Phase III transition rate" is not rigorous for this technology area. Procurement, private investment, acquisition, patents, and subcontracting each illuminate different outcomes. Agencies should report them separately and state the evidence channel behind each number.

## Finding 1: Nanotechnology has two visible commercialization pathways, not one average outcome

DoD and DOE nanotechnology awards are more likely to reappear in federal procurement records. NSF-funded nanotechnology firms are more likely to raise private capital. These are different commercialization pathways, not competing versions of the same outcome.

| Agency | Nanotech Phase II awards | FPDS-coded Phase III | Post-Phase-II Form D | Either signal |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| Department of Defense | 1,413 | 12.5% | 8.8% | 19.4% |
| Department of Energy | 364 | 11.3% | 6.3% | 17.3% |
| NASA | 258 | 8.1% | 7.8% | 14.7% |
| Health and Human Services | 393 | 3.1% | 9.4% | 12.0% |
| National Science Foundation | 333 | 2.4% | 17.7% | 19.2% |

Only 37 awards (1.3% of the cohort) show both FPDS-coded Phase III evidence and a post-Phase-II Form D filing — a substantive result: firms commercializing through government procurement are usually not the same firms commercializing through venture-backed private markets.

**Policy interpretation.** A single nanotechnology transition rate would mix defense procurement, biomedical commercialization, energy applications, and platform technologies into one misleading average. Reporting should distinguish at least two pathways: federal-customer continuation and private-market capitalization.

## Finding 2: Acquisition evidence is high value but structurally undercounted

The full report identifies 11 nanotechnology SBIR firms with documentary evidence of acquisition by defense, pharma, or medtech primes — a small share of the cohort, but strong evidence: an acquirer is paying for technology, talent, IP, or delivery capacity.

| SBIR firm | Acquirer | Domain | SEC-disclosed value |
|---|---|---|---:|
| Physical Optics Corporation | Mercury Systems | Defense electronics and photonics | ~$310M |
| Anasys Instruments Corp | Bruker | Nano-IR and AFM-IR instrumentation | ~$32.3M |
| EraGen Biosciences | Luminex | Molecular diagnostics | ~$34M |
| Visen Medical, Inc. | PerkinElmer | In vivo fluorescence imaging | ~$23M |

The leverage multiple differs by firm type. Mature defense contractors can be acquired below cumulative SBIR funding because much of the government value was already delivered through contracts. IP-rich biomedical and instrumentation firms can be acquired at several times cumulative SBIR funding because the acquirer is purchasing a scalable asset. Acquisition timelines also run long — the technical appendix's Finding 2 gives the specific medians and outliers.

**Rigor caveat.** The 11 acquisitions are a floor, not a full count. Acquisitions by private firms, private-equity-owned primes, renamed entities, or transactions below SEC reporting thresholds are often invisible to EDGAR-based methods.

## Finding 3: "No signal" is not the same as "no commercialization"

The most important measurement problem is the large indeterminate population. Among mature awards without FPDS-coded Phase III activity:

| Indeterminate reason | Awards | Share of indeterminate group |
|---|---:|---:|
| No federal activity record post-award | 1,298 | 54.7% |
| No UEI link to federal systems | 539 | 22.7% |
| Firm appears in FPDS, but no contract is Phase III coded | 354 | 14.9% |
| Non-DoD agency where FPDS coding is sparse | 182 | 7.7% |

This taxonomy is more rigorous than treating all missing evidence as failure. The technical appendix goes further: cross-referencing patents, trademarks, subawards, clinical-trial and FDA registries, and corporate rename records finds continuing activity for roughly two in three of the firms with no federal activity record, with a statistical bound suggesting the true share may run closer to four in five. One example — a firm absorbed into a defense prime under a new name, invisible to every SEC-filing-based check — illustrates why identity persistence, not just identity absence, is part of the gap. The remaining unresolved minority is where a targeted survey belongs (technical appendix, Finding 3).

**Policy interpretation.** The right next step is not a broad survey of all firms. It is a triage ladder: fix entity resolution, recover unflagged contract evidence, add public-record signals, and only then survey the residual group that remains dark.

## Finding 4: FPDS Phase III coding cannot support cross-agency rankings

FPDS Phase III coding is most useful for DoD-heavy procurement pathways. It is not a neutral cross-agency outcome metric — NIH, NSF, DOE, and other agencies may support commercialization through grants, cooperative agreements, follow-on R&D, private capital, licenses, or acquisitions that FPDS's Phase III field does not consistently capture.

Contract-level recovery matters here. For 536 awards whose firms appeared in federal records but lacked Phase III flags, post-award USAspending review found:

| Evidence tier | Meaning | Awards |
|---|---|---:|
| Strong | Explicit Phase III text or same-agency non-SBIR contract of at least $25K | 301 |
| Moderate | Other non-SBIR federal activity | 159 |
| Weak | Later SBIR/STTR only | 72 |
| None | No post-award federal actions | 4 |

The absence of the Phase III flag is often a coding gap, not an absence of follow-on activity.

**Policy interpretation.** FPDS-coded Phase III should be reported as a conservative floor. Cross-agency dashboards should include separate columns for coded Phase III, unflagged federal follow-on obligations, private capital, patents, acquisitions, and subawards.

## Finding 5: Patents help validate the cohort and reveal outcomes that procurement data misses

The patent analysis (CPC B82Y/B82B classes from PatentsView) identifies 481 SBIR Phase II firms with at least one nanotechnology-classified patent. This should not replace the award-text cohort — it is firm-grained, so a firm may hold nanotechnology patents while a particular SBIR award was in another domain.

The patent lens is useful three ways:

- **Validation.** It tests whether the keyword cohort is meaningfully nanotechnology-related.
- **Timing.** About half of patent-holding firms filed their first nanotechnology application after the first Phase II award year, suggesting post-award capability development; pre-award filers instead indicate existing capability.
- **Signal discovery.** Patent-holding firms show stronger private-capital and M&A signals than non-holders, while FPDS Phase III rates are similar at the firm level.

**Rigor caveat.** Patents are an imperfect signal. Small firms may not patent, examiners may under-assign B82 classes, and exact-name assignee matching misses subsidiaries, renames, and university-linked commercialization paths.

## What policy leaders should take away

1. **Do not ask for one nanotechnology SBIR transition rate.** Ask for channel-specific outcomes with stated coverage limits.
2. **Do not compare NSF or NIH to DoD using FPDS-coded Phase III alone.** That comparison rewards procurement-system visibility rather than commercialization performance.
3. **Use longer evaluation windows.** Private raises and acquisitions often arrive several years after Phase II; short annual or biennial windows undercount deep-tech outcomes.
4. **Treat identity resolution as infrastructure.** Retroactive UEI backfill and successor-name tracking would recover evidence without imposing new reporting burdens on firms.
5. **Make subawards and patents standard evidence channels.** These signals are public, machine-readable, and especially useful where prime-contract records are weak.
6. **Reserve surveys for the unresolved residual.** Administrative and public-record checks should narrow the population before agencies impose survey burden.

## Language to avoid when summarizing these findings

Further compressions of this brief (briefing slides, press materials, follow-up memos) should avoid these phrases unless narrowed:

- Avoid: "the nanotechnology transition rate is 17.4%."
  Use: "17.4% of awards show one of the two strict flag-based signals measured here."
- Avoid: "71.9% did not commercialize."
  Use: "71.9% remain indeterminate after the recovery checks applied here."
- Avoid: "patents prove commercialization."
  Use: "post-award patent filings are commercialization-adjacent evidence and a useful signal for technology development."
- Avoid: "FPDS shows agency performance."
  Use: "FPDS-coded Phase III is a procurement-system signal whose coverage differs sharply by agency."
